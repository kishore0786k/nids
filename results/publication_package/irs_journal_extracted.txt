Abstract—Intelligent reflecting surfaces (IRSs) offer a promis-ing way to control wireless propagation, but their practical performance is often limited by imperfect channel knowledge and non-ideal hardware behavior. In realistic deployments, factors such as channel estimation errors, discrete phase shifts, phase noise, amplitude attenuation, and imperfect successive interfer-ence cancellation can collectively degrade the effectiveness of IRS-based optimization.
In this work, we study a downlink IRS-assisted non-orthogonal multiple access (NOMA) system under these practical constraints, while also considering the presence of a passive eavesdropper. Unlike idealized models, the system explicitly captures multiple sources of uncertainty and hardware imperfections within a unified framework, enabling a more realistic evaluation of IRS-assisted secure communication.
A secrecy-oriented robust IRS optimization framework is pro-posed, where the phase configuration is obtained using a sample-average projected coordinate ascent method. Unlike conventional designs that focus solely on maximizing the legitimate-link rate, the proposed approach explicitly incorporates a secrecy-aware objective, enabling a controlled tradeoff between throughput and information leakage.
Simulation results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves a secrecy rate of 10.526 bps/Hz, corresponding to gains of 13.26% and 13.86% over alternating optimization and greedy alignment baselines, respectively, while incurring only marginal sum-rate reductions of 1.19% and 2.35%. Furthermore, the secrecy advantage remains consistent across varying CSI error levels, confirming the robustness of the approach.
These results indicate that IRS optimization strategies designed with explicit secrecy objectives can provide meaningful perfor-mance gains in practical, impairment-limited wireless environ-ments.

ROGRAMMABLE wireless environments have evolved from a conceptual idea to a practical design paradigm, where the propagation medium itself can be actively con-trolled. Early demonstrations of software-defined metasurfaces showed that electromagnetic reflections can be dynamically adjusted, enabling environment-aware communication strate-gies [1]. This concept was later formalized in wireless sys-tems through joint active and passive beamforming, which
positioned intelligent reflecting surfaces (IRSs) as a key opti-mization component in modern networks [2].
As the field matured, attention shifted toward more realistic system considerations. Energy-efficient IRS designs revealed the impact of discrete phase shifts and hardware limitations on achievable performance [3]. Broader studies further ex-amined system-level challenges, including modeling accuracy, implementation tradeoffs, and integration into existing network architectures [4]. Subsequent works provided deeper insights into signal processing aspects and practical deployment issues in IRS-assisted communication [5]. Analytical frameworks were also developed to better capture propagation character-istics under realistic conditions [6], while survey efforts high-lighted scalability and standardization challenges that remain open [7].
Despite these developments, many existing studies rely on simplified assumptions that limit their practical relevance. In real deployments, imperfect channel state information (CSI) introduces uncertainty in IRS configuration [8], while hard-ware impairments such as phase noise and non-ideal reflection characteristics degrade the effectiveness of phase control [9]. In addition, finite-resolution phase shifters introduce quanti-zation errors that restrict achievable beamforming gains [10], and discrete phase models have been shown to produce noticeable performance gaps compared to ideal continuous-phase designs [11]. Environmental factors such as path loss variations further complicate IRS optimization [12].
A key limitation of the existing literature is that these impairments are typically studied in isolation. In practice, they coexist and interact, leading to compounded performance degradation. Designing IRS control strategies under such combined effects is therefore essential for realistic system evaluation.
A similar gap is observed in IRS-assisted non-orthogonal multiple access (NOMA) systems. Early works primarily focused on improving spectral efficiency or reducing compu-tational complexity [13], often without considering robustness aspects [14]. Extensions to millimeter-wave scenarios high-lighted beam alignment challenges but still relied on idealized assumptions [15].
From a security perspective, IRS-assisted transmission has
been explored using artificial noise, secrecy rate maximization, and outage-based formulations [16]. More advanced studies considered secure NOMA transmission under simplified con-ditions [17], while robust secrecy designs typically address only partial uncertainty models [18]. Emerging architectures such as STAR-IRS further expand the design space but still lack comprehensive evaluation under multiple impair-ments [19].
Overall, existing work rarely examines secrecy performance under a joint set of practical impairments, raising concerns about the real-world applicability of reported gains.
This paper addresses this gap by developing a secrecy-oriented IRS-NOMA framework that explicitly incorporates multiple non-idealities. Rather than maximizing throughput under ideal assumptions, the focus is on maintaining reliable secrecy performance in realistic conditions. The main contri-butions are summarized as follows:
A unified downlink IRS-NOMA model that jointly ac-counts for imperfect CSI, amplitude loss, phase noise, finite phase resolution, residual SIC, and an eavesdrop-per.
A robust IRS configuration method based on projected coordinate ascent with stochastic sample averaging.
A comparative evaluation against representative base-lines, including alternating optimization (AO), greedy alignment, random phase control, direct transmission, and quantized IRS schemes.
A detailed analysis of the secrecy–rate tradeoff, high-lighting the impact of practical impairments on system performance.
Unlike prior studies, this work evaluates secrecy perfor-mance under a combined impairment setting, providing a more realistic assessment of IRS-assisted secure communication.
Related Work and Problem Positioning
Research on intelligent reflecting surfaces (IRS) has pro-gressed rapidly, with early efforts focusing on improving received signal strength and spectral efficiency through joint active and passive beamforming [2]. This direction was later extended to energy-efficient designs, where the influence of practical constraints such as finite-resolution phase shifts be-came more evident [3]. System-level studies further examined how IRS can be integrated into future wireless networks, identifying both architectural benefits and implementation challenges [4]. In parallel, tutorial-style works provided a broader understanding of signal processing techniques and practical design considerations for IRS-assisted systems [5].
As the focus shifted toward practical deployment, robust-ness emerged as a central challenge. Imperfect channel state information (CSI) has been shown to significantly affect the reliability of IRS phase optimization, as the design depends directly on estimated rather than exact channel conditions [8]. In addition to channel uncertainty, hardware-related impair-ments further degrade performance. Phase noise and non-ideal reflection characteristics can distort the intended signal shaping, limiting the effectiveness of IRS-based control in realistic scenarios [9].
Another important constraint arises from the use of discrete phase shifts in low-cost IRS implementations. Finite phase resolution restricts the ability to precisely align reflected signals, leading to performance degradation compared to ideal continuous-phase designs [10]. This effect becomes more pronounced in high-precision beamforming settings, where quantization introduces a noticeable gap in achievable perfor-mance [11].
At the same time, IRS-assisted secure communication has received increasing attention. Early studies demonstrated that IRS can improve secrecy by shaping the propagation envi-ronment to favor legitimate receivers [16]. This concept was later extended to NOMA systems, where secure transmis-sion must also account for user ordering and interference management [17]. More recent approaches have attempted to incorporate robustness into secrecy design, although most existing works consider only a limited set of impairments or simplified uncertainty models [18].
Despite these advances, a key limitation remains. In much of the existing literature, robustness and security are treated as separate design objectives. Practical factors such as CSI errors, phase quantization, and hardware impairments are often analyzed in isolation, while secrecy-oriented designs typically assume ideal or partially simplified conditions. In real deployments, however, these effects occur simultaneously and interact in ways that can significantly alter system behavior. As a result, methods that appear effective under idealized assump-tions may not retain their advantages in realistic environments. This work addresses that gap by considering a unified setting in which secrecy performance is evaluated under com-bined practical constraints. Instead of focusing on ideal rate maximization, the objective is to examine whether a secrecy-oriented IRS design can maintain a consistent advantage when both channel uncertainty and hardware imperfections are present. The results show that such robustness is achievable, although it introduces a measurable tradeoff in nominal sum rate. This secrecy–throughput tradeoff forms the central design
perspective of the paper.

In addition, the presence of channel uncertainty further complicates the design, as the optimal phase configuration depends on imperfect channel estimates rather than exact values. As a result, directly solving the problem for a single channel realization can lead to unreliable performance.
To overcome these challenges, we adopt an iterative ap-proach that combines sample-average approximation with a projectedcoordinate ascent.
Motivation
Conventional IRS optimization approaches, such as alter-nating optimization (AO), typically focus on maximizing the legitimate link gain while ignoring the impact on unintended receivers. These methods also rely on deterministic channel estimates, which leads to performance degradation under im-perfect CSI.
In contrast, the proposed method explicitly incorporates both secrecy considerations and channel uncertainty. By opti-mizing a statistical objective rather than a single realization, the design becomes more resilient to estimation errors and hardware imperfections.

Algorithm Summary
The proposed algorithm updates the IRS configuration it-eratively by cycling through all reflecting elements. During each iteration, the phase of every element is refined using the derived update rule and then projected onto the discrete phase set through quantization.
Through this iterative refinement, the secrecy-aware objec-tive improves progressively while ensuring that all practical constraints are satisfied.

The wireless channels are modeled using Rician fading with distance-dependent path loss and log-normal shadowing. Spatial correlation is incorporated in the IRS links to reflect realistic propagation conditions. Imperfect CSI is introduced through additive complex Gaussian estimation errors, while hardware impairments are modeled via phase noise and am-plitude loss at the IRS elements. Residual interference due to imperfect SIC is also included in the NOMA model.
The system parameters are summarized in Table I. Unless otherwise stated, the IRS consists of N = 64 reflecting elements, and the base station serves K = 3 users at a carrier frequency of 3.5 GHz. The IRS operates with finite-resolution phase shifts, and the system model simultaneously incorpo-rates channel estimation errors and hardware impairments.
These settings are chosen to reflect a practical mid-scale deployment scenario, where both propagation uncertainty and non-ideal hardware effects play a significant role in determin-ing system performance.
The evaluation considers several baseline schemes to pro-vide a comprehensive comparison. These include a literature-inspired alternating optimization (AO) method focused on maximizing the legitimate link, a greedy phase alignment strategy, and a random IRS configuration as a reference. In addition, direct-link transmission without IRS assistance and fixed quantized IRS schemes are included to reflect practical deployment scenarios.
The presented results focus on the key performance aspects relevant to practical deployment. These include the tradeoff between secrecy and throughput, secrecy scaling with the number of IRS elements, robustness to CSI errors, incremental
gain over greedy alignment, and statistical stability as observed through convergence and distribution-level analysis.

Limitations
The presented results are based on a simulation-driven evaluation and do not include over-the-air experimentation or hardware prototyping of the IRS. In addition, the comparator schemes are implemented as representative baselines rather than exact reproductions of specific published algorithms.
The proposed controller is designed to prioritize secrecy performance, which results in a modest reduction in nominal sum rate compared to throughput-oriented baselines. This tradeoff reflects the underlying design objective and highlights the practical balance between throughput and confidentiality in IRS-assisted systems.
Conclusion
This paper investigated a secrecy-oriented robust IRS design for downlink NOMA systems under imperfect CSI and prac-tical hardware impairments. A sample-average optimization framework combined with projected coordinate updates was developed to jointly enhance the legitimate signal and suppress information leakage.
Simulation results demonstrate that the proposed method consistently improves secrecy performance over representative baselines, particularly in the presence of channel uncertainty. While this improvement is accompanied by a modest reduc-tion in nominal sum rate, the results highlight a clear and controllable tradeoff between throughput and confidentiality.
These findings demonstrate that robustness-aware IRS de-sign is essential for translating theoretical gains into practical secure communication systems.
Overall, the study shows that incorporating robustness and secrecy-awareness into IRS design leads to more reliable performance under realistic conditions, providing a practical direction for secure IRS-assisted wireless systems.
